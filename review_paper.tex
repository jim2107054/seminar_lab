\begin{abstract}
\noindent
The integration of reinforcement learning (RL) with multimodal large language models
(MLLMs) has opened a transformative avenue for autonomous medical image analysis.
Three recent systems --- IBISAgent~\cite{ibisagent}, MedVistaGym (together with
MedVista-R1)~\cite{medvistagym}, and MedSAM-Agent~\cite{medsam_agent} --- represent
the current frontier of this research direction, each offering a distinct perspective
on how intelligent agents can perform pixel-level segmentation and clinical reasoning
without direct human intervention.  This survey paper provides a rigorous and
comparative analysis of the conceptual architecture, training methodology, reward
design, experimental validation, and broader clinical implications of these three
frameworks.  We examine shared design principles --- notably the reformulation of
segmentation as a Markov Decision Process, the use of two-stage supervised fine-tuning
followed by RL, and the exploitation of tool-integrated feedback loops --- alongside
key differentiating factors such as action-space formulation, reward granularity,
dataset diversity, and out-of-domain generalization.  Through this analysis we identify
open challenges including computational overhead, scalability to volumetric 3D data,
and the need for richer process-level supervision.  The review concludes with a
synthesis of lessons and a forward-looking perspective on the next generation of
agentic medical AI.

\vspace{6pt}
\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} Medical Image Segmentation, Multimodal Large Language
Models, Reinforcement Learning, Agentic AI, Segment Anything Model, Interactive
Segmentation, Markov Decision Process, Tool-Integrated Reasoning.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Medical image segmentation occupies a foundational position in contemporary clinical
practice.  The capacity to isolate anatomical structures, delineate pathological
lesions, and quantify morphological change underpins decisions in oncology, radiology,
cardiology, and surgical planning.  Historically, this task has been addressed through
two distinct paradigms: task-specific supervised models such as U-Net~\cite{unet} and
its successors, and interactive segmentation tools like the Segment Anything
Model~\cite{sam} and its medical adaptations~\cite{medsam2}.  Both paradigms carry
inherent limitations that constrain deployment at scale.

Supervised models achieve impressive benchmarks within the narrow distributions they
are trained on, yet they degrade markedly when presented with out-of-distribution
imaging modalities or unexpected pathology patterns.  Interactive models such as
MedSAM~\cite{medsam2} resolve the distribution shift problem through
prompt-conditioned inference, but they transfer the cognitive burden to clinicians who
must supply bounding boxes or click prompts for every case --- an arrangement that is
operationally unsustainable in high-volume clinical settings.

The emergence of MLLMs has created a third possibility: systems that can read natural
language instructions, interpret visual content, and take actions --- replacing the
human prompter with an autonomous reasoning agent.  Pioneering work such as
LISA~\cite{lisa} demonstrated that a single \texttt{<SEG>} token embedded in the MLLM
output stream could be decoded by an external pixel decoder to generate segmentation
masks.  While conceptually elegant, this approach introduces implicit tokens that
disrupt the model's native language representation space and forces joint fine-tuning
of the language model and the decoder, raising the risk of catastrophic forgetting and
limiting cross-domain generalization~\cite{ibisagent}.

A more recent line of research argues that segmentation should be conceptualized not
as a single forward pass but as an iterative, agent-environment interaction cycle.
Three independent systems published within months of one another in early 2026 embody
this philosophy: IBISAgent~\cite{ibisagent}, which reframes biomedical object referring
and segmentation as a multi-step Markov Decision Process (MDP) driven by click-based
spatial prompts; MedVistaGym~\cite{medvistagym}, which establishes a scalable training
environment and uses it to train MedVista-R1, an agent that invokes heterogeneous
visual and biomedical knowledge tools through structured tool calls; and
MedSAM-Agent~\cite{medsam_agent}, which extends the interactive segmentation paradigm
with a hybrid box-and-point action space and a clinically motivated process reward
design.

Despite sharing the broad goal of autonomous medical image segmentation, these three
systems differ substantially in their action-space formulations, reward structures,
training data scales, and evaluation protocols.  A comparative study is therefore not
merely an academic exercise; it offers practitioners and researchers a structured
understanding of the trade-offs involved in deploying agentic segmentation systems and
illuminates the open problems that the field must address.

Section~\ref{sec:discussion} provides a structured discussion.
Section~\ref{sec:synthesis} provides an integrative synthesis and identifies future
frontiers.  Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes.

\section{Background and Related Work}
\label{sec:background}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{Evolution of Medical Image Segmentation}

The trajectory of medical image segmentation research over the past decade reflects a
tension between specificity and generality.  Methods grounded in fully convolutional
networks such as U-Net~\cite{unet} established strong intra-dataset performance but
required retraining for each new task.  The introduction of nnU-Net~\cite{nnunet}
automated the training pipeline but preserved the fundamental constraint of
task-specific parameter sets.  Transformer-based architectures extended the receptive
field and improved handling of long-range spatial dependencies, yet the core paradigm
remained intact.

The release of SAM~\cite{sam} marked a conceptual departure by offering a promptable,
task-agnostic segmentation foundation.  SAM accepts points, bounding boxes, or masks
as prompts and produces high-quality segmentation across diverse natural image domains.
MedSAM~\cite{medsam2} adapted this architecture to biomedical imaging through
fine-tuning on large-scale annotated medical datasets, demonstrating substantial gains
over zero-shot SAM.  MedSAM2~\cite{medsam2} extended the approach to 3D volumes and
video sequences.  These models, however, remain dependent on expert-supplied prompts
at inference time.

\subsection{MLLMs for Pixel-Level Understanding}

The integration of MLLMs into pixel-level visual understanding was substantially
advanced by LISA~\cite{lisa}, which introduced a reasoning segmentation task wherein
the model must parse a complex natural language query and generate the corresponding
segmentation mask.  The mechanism relies on a special \texttt{<SEG>} embedding whose
hidden states are decoded by a SAM-like pixel decoder.  This paradigm was subsequently
adapted to the medical domain by several groups.  MedPLIB~\cite{medplib} extended LISA
with pixel-level insight for biomedicine; UniBiomed~\cite{unibiomed} constructed a
universal biomedical foundation model covering grounded image interpretation; and
Citrus-V~\cite{citrusv} advanced unified medical grounding for clinical reasoning.

A critical analysis of these LISA-style systems reveals two structural limitations.
First, the introduction of implicit tokens into the output stream alters the model's
language generation distribution, compromising its capacity for open-ended reasoning.
Second, all of these approaches are fundamentally single-turn: given a question, the
model performs one forward pass and emits a mask.  This architecture cannot support
iterative refinement, and therefore cannot emulate the multi-step annotation workflows
that human experts employ in clinical practice~\cite{ibisagent,medsam_agent}.

\subsection{Tool-Augmented and Agentic Reasoning}

The concept of augmenting language models with external tools has a long history in
general natural language processing, but its application to medical visual reasoning
has accelerated significantly following the success of DeepSeek-R1~\cite{deepseekr1}
and related RL-based reasoning frameworks.  In the medical domain,
MMedAgent~\cite{mmedagent} constructed an instruction-tuning corpus that trained an
MLLM to invoke specialized medical tools in a single step.  VILA-M3~\cite{vilam3}
triggered expert perception models for specific subtasks.  AURA~\cite{aura} integrated
heterogeneous tools for medical VQA.  These approaches treat tool usage as a fixed
function call rather than as part of a dynamic, feedback-driven reasoning loop.

The three systems reviewed in this paper ---
IBISAgent~\cite{ibisagent}, MedVistaGym/MedVista-R1~\cite{medvistagym}, and
MedSAM-Agent~\cite{medsam_agent} --- all move beyond single-turn tool invocation
toward multi-turn, RL-optimized interaction.  They share the key insight that effective
tool use in clinical visual reasoning requires learning disciplined interaction
policies, not merely having access to tools.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{IBISAgent: Iterative Biomedical Interactive Segmentation Agent}
\label{sec:ibis}

\subsubsection{Conceptual Foundation}

IBISAgent~\cite{ibisagent} was designed by researchers at Zhejiang University and the
Shanghai Artificial Intelligence Laboratory to resolve two structural deficiencies of
existing medical MLLMs: the reliance on implicit segmentation tokens that corrupts the
language representation space, and the restriction to single-pass inference that
prevents iterative mask refinement.  The core insight motivating IBISAgent is that
segmentation can be recast as a multi-step MDP in which the MLLM generates text-based
click commands, invokes an external interactive segmentation tool (specifically
MedSAM2~\cite{medsam2}), and updates its action plan based on the resulting mask
observation.

This formulation preserves the MLLM's native text output space entirely.  Rather than
emitting a \texttt{<SEG>} token, the model outputs structured action strings that
encode the target class, click polarity (positive or negative), and normalized 2D
coordinate.  The interaction continues until the model judges the mask to be
satisfactory, at which point it emits a termination signal and generates a natural
language answer.  Crucially, no architectural modifications to the base MLLM
(Qwen2.5-VL-7B~\cite{qwen25vl}) are required beyond training.

  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{fig2.png}
  \caption{Overview of the IBISAgent framework showing (a)~the overall agent

   (b)~the cold-start SFT pipeline using Gemini-2.5-Pro and GPT-5, and

\subsubsection{Architecture and Rollout}

Formally, IBISAgent represents an interaction trajectory as
$\mathcal{P} = \{(r_t, a_t, o_t)\}_{t=1}^{T}$, where $r_t$ is the textual reasoning
at step $t$, $a_t$ is the click action, and $o_t$ is the segmentation observation ---
a composite image in which the current mask is overlaid semi-transparently on the
original image.  This composite observation is re-encoded by the vision encoder at
each step, enabling the model to simultaneously perceive both the current mask
boundaries and the underlying anatomy.  The iterative thought-action-observation loop
continues until a designated end token is produced or a maximum context length is
reached.

The action space captures three components for each click: \textit{Target} (the
segmentation class name), \textit{Attribute} ($\{+1, -1\}$), and
\textit{Coordinate\_2d} ($(x, y) \in [0, 1]^2$).  The observation $o_0$ is
initialized as an empty mask, and the final observation represents the optimal target
mask.

\subsubsection{Dataset Construction and Trajectory Generation}

A central challenge in building IBISAgent was the absence of publicly available
step-wise annotation trajectories.  The authors addressed this through an automated
click simulation algorithm applied to the large-scale BioMedParseData
corpus~\cite{biomedparse}, which contains 3.4 million image-mask-label tuples covering
82 biomedical object types across 9 imaging modalities.  Given the ground truth mask
$M_{\text{gt}}$ and the current predicted mask $M_t$, the simulator identifies false
positive (FP) and false negative (FN) regions, applies a Euclidean distance transform,
and places the next click at the centroid of the largest error region --- a strategy
adapted from prior interactive segmentation work~\cite{deepinteractive}.

Gemini-2.5-Pro~\cite{gemini25} was employed to generate diverse question-answer pairs.
GPT-5~\cite{gpt5} was subsequently used to synthesize step-by-step reasoning traces
for each click action via a Teacher-Student pipeline, producing anatomically grounded
reasoning without revealing ground truth information in the generated text.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{fig3.png}
  \caption{Automated trajectory generation for liver segmentation in IBISAgent.  The
   algorithm places positive clicks (blue) in false-negative regions and negative
   clicks (red) in false-positive regions, progressively improving IoU from 0 to
   $\approx$0.95 across seven interaction steps.
   Adapted from Jiang et al.~\cite{ibisagent}.}
  \label{fig:3}
\end{figure}

An additional layer of data augmentation introduced self-reflective trajectories
covering two error-correction scenarios: self-correction (where the agent detects a
wrong action and reverts) and user-inconsistency correction (where the initial mask
provided by the user does not match the instruction and must be discarded).  The
resulting cold-start SFT dataset $\mathcal{D}_\text{cold}$ comprises 456K samples
with an average trajectory length of 8.69 steps and an overall average IoU of 94.27.

\subsubsection{Two-Stage Training}

IBISAgent is trained in two stages.  The first stage, cold-start SFT, trains the model
to generate syntactically valid action sequences, produce anatomically grounded
reasoning, and execute self-correction behavior.  The SFT loss is a standard negative
log-likelihood over reasoning and action tokens.

The second stage applies Group Relative Policy Optimization
(GRPO)~\cite{deepseekr1} on the RL dataset $\mathcal{D}_\text{rl}$, which contains
888K VQA samples including both pixel-level reasoning tasks and general medical VQA
data.  The reward function is a normalized sum of five components:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Format reward} $S_\text{format}$ --- checks structural output validity;
  \item \textbf{Final-answer reward} $S_\text{ans}$ --- measures IoU against ground
        truth with piecewise thresholds;
  \item \textbf{Region-based click placement reward} $S_\text{click}$ --- penalizes
        positive clicks outside FN regions and negative clicks outside FP regions;
  \item \textbf{Progressive improvement reward} $S_\text{pseg} = \mathbb{1}(\Delta\text{IoU}_t > 0)$ --- rewards each action yielding a strictly positive IoU gain;
  \item \textbf{Trajectory length reward} $S_\text{len}$ --- penalizes sequences
        exceeding a predefined optimal length $T_\text{opt}$.
\end{enumerate}
The final reward is $S = \frac{1}{5}(S_\text{ans} + S_\text{format} +
S_\text{click} + S_\text{pseg} + S_\text{len})$.

\subsubsection{Experimental Results}

IBISAgent is evaluated on three benchmarks: an in-domain test set
$\mathcal{D}_\text{test}$ (9K image-mask pairs from BioMedParseData), the
out-of-domain MeCOVQA-G+ benchmark~\cite{medplib} (3,157 pairs across 5 modalities),
and a held-out in-house dataset comprising 1K CT, MRI, and histopathology images
across 7 cancer types.  On the in-domain benchmark, IBISAgent achieves IoU~= 85.58\%,
DSC~= 92.21\%, and F1~= 96.39\%, surpassing the next best method
(UniBiomed~\cite{unibiomed} with IoU~= 50.74\%) by approximately 35 percentage
points.  On MeCOVQA-G+, IBISAgent attains IoU~= 80.63\%, compared to 46.54\% for
Citrus-V~\cite{citrusv}.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{fig4.png}
  \caption{Qualitative comparison on a hepatic tumor segmentation case.  GPT-4o
   misidentifies the location; MedPLIB outputs only a \texttt{<SEG>} token;
   UniBiomed hallucinates non-existent findings.  IBISAgent iteratively refines the
   mask across four steps, reaching IoU~= 96.74\% with coherent clinical reasoning.

  \label{fig:4}

Ablation studies confirm that RL training provides the largest single performance
gain, and that each of the five reward components makes an independent contribution to
both segmentation quality and interaction efficiency.

\subsection{MedVistaGym and MedVista-R1: Tool-Integrated Reasoning for Medical VQA}
\label{sec:medvista}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsubsection{Conceptual Foundation}

The MedVistaGym framework~\cite{medvistagym}, developed by researchers at Virginia
Tech, UT Southwestern Medical Center, Georgia Institute of Technology, and Cisco,
targets a complementary challenge: enabling VLMs to reason about medical images by
dynamically selecting and invoking heterogeneous external tools, rather than being
limited to a fixed segmentation backend.  The central claim is that effective
tool-integrated reasoning in the medical domain cannot be achieved through tool access
alone --- it requires explicit training of disciplined interaction policies through RL.

MedVistaGym is constructed as a scalable, executable Gym-style environment that
provides VLMs with access to 15 visual and biomedical knowledge tools organized into
four families: (1)~Resolution and Region Refinement (e.g., 4KAgent); (2)~Medical
Localization and Segmentation (e.g., GroundingDINO, SAM2, BioMedParse, MedSAM2);
(3)~Medical Visual Understanding and Parsing (e.g., BiomedCLIP); and (4)~External
Biomedical Knowledge Retrieval (e.g., PubMed, DrugBank, GoogleSearch).

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{fig5.png}
  \caption{Overview of the MEDVISTAGYM environment: bio-medical images feed into an
   LLM agent that interacts with a suite of visual tools and produces annotated
   outputs; the right panel illustrates a chest X-ray pneumonia analysis use case.
   Adapted from Lu et al.~\cite{medvistagym}.}
  \label{fig:5}
\end{figure}

\subsubsection{Problem Formulation}

MedVistaGym formalizes tool-integrated medical image analysis as a Partially
Observable Markov Decision Process (POMDP).  Given a medical question with associated
image(s), the agent generates explicit reasoning steps and selects tool actions from a
well-defined, executable action space.  An interaction trajectory
$U = (g_0, a_0, \ldots, g_T, \hat{y})$ records interleaved reasoning steps $g_t$,
tool actions $a_t$, and the final answer $\hat{y}$ after $T$ interaction steps.  The
strict output format ---
\texttt{<think>}\,$\ldots$\,\texttt{</think>}%
\texttt{<tool\_call>}\,$\ldots$\,\texttt{</tool\_call>}%
\texttt{<think>}\,$\ldots$\,\texttt{</think>}%
\texttt{<answer>}\,$\ldots$\,\texttt{</answer>} ---
enforces a sequential discipline: the agent must reason before calling a tool, then
reason again after observing the output before emitting a final answer.

\subsubsection{Training Data and Trajectory Generation}

Training data for MedVista-R1 is drawn from PathVQA~\cite{pathvqa},
SLAKE~\cite{slake}, and VQA-RAD~\cite{vqarad}.  GPT-5~\cite{gpt5} is deployed within
the live MEDVISTAGYM environment to generate candidate reasoning trajectories with real
tool invocations, retaining only those whose final answers match the ground truth.  A
second GPT-5 pass assigns ordinal quality scores on a 4-point scale based on reasoning
coherence, appropriate tool selection, and integration of tool outputs.

Unlike IBISAgent, which constructs trajectories from automated click simulation on
mask annotations, MedVistaGym produces trajectories through live interaction with
deployed tool services --- capturing the stochastic and imperfect nature of real tool
outputs.

\subsubsection{Training and Reward Design}

MedVista-R1 is trained on InternVL3 (2B and 8B variants) in two stages.  Stage~I uses
behavioral cloning to establish reliable tool invocation syntax.  Stage~II applies
GRPO~\cite{deepseekr1} within MEDVISTAGYM.  The reward function comprises three
components:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Format reward} $R_\text{format}$ --- verifies all required special
        tokens appear in correct order;
  \item \textbf{Accuracy reward} $R_\text{acc} = \mathbb{1}[\hat{y} = y]$ --- binary
        match between predicted and ground-truth answer;
  \item \textbf{Conditional tool-use reward} $R_\text{tool}$ --- granted only when the
        trajectory invokes at least one external tool \emph{and} produces a correct
        final answer.
\end{enumerate}
The total rollout reward is $R(U) = R_\text{format}(U) + R_\text{acc}(U) + R_\text{tool}(U)$.

\subsubsection{Experimental Results}

MedVista-R1-8B achieves 73.70\% accuracy on the in-domain average, outperforming
comparably-sized tool-augmented baselines without RL by 19.10--24.21\%.  On the
overall average across all six benchmarks, MedVista-R1-8B achieves 63.09\%, compared
to 54.66\% for the vanilla InternVL3-8B backbone and 38.88\% for InternVL3-8B with
tool access but without RL training.

\subsection{MedSAM-Agent: Multi-turn Agentic RL for Interactive Segmentation}
\label{sec:medsam}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsubsection{Conceptual Foundation}

MedSAM-Agent~\cite{medsam_agent}, developed by researchers at the Chinese University
of Hong Kong, Tencent's Hunyuan Group, the Institute of Automation (Chinese Academy of
Sciences), Dalian University of Technology, and Stanford University, addresses two
limitations identified in prior agentic segmentation work: the restriction of the
action space to sequential point clicks, which lacks spatial flexibility for
morphologically heterogeneous structures, and the exclusive focus on terminal-outcome
rewards, which provides no process-level guidance on interaction efficiency.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{fig7.png}
  \caption{Overview of the MedSAM-Agent framework: trajectory generation with hybrid
   Box-to-Point and Sequential-Click strategies, two-stage training pipeline, and
   fine-grained reward design.
   Adapted from Liu et al.~\cite{medsam_agent}.}
  \label{fig:7}
\end{figure}

\subsubsection{Action Space and Observation}

The MedSAM-Agent action space covers three primitive operations:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{\texttt{add\_bbox}} --- $[x_1, y_1, x_2, y_2]$ bounding box for global
        spatial context;
  \item \textbf{\texttt{add\_point}} --- $[x, y]$ with polarity $\alpha \in \{+1, -1\}$
        for local refinement;
  \item \textbf{\texttt{stop\_action}} --- signals that the mask meets clinical quality
        standards.
\end{itemize}
The observation $o_t$ is the updated predicted mask $M_t$ generated by calling the
segmentation backend (MedSAM2~\cite{medsam2} or IMISNet~\cite{imisnet}).  A key
architectural decision is the omission of a dedicated \texttt{<think>} token during
RL, forcing the model to internalize spatial reasoning directly within action
generation.

\subsubsection{Hybrid Trajectory Generation}

The trajectory generation algorithm implements two parallel paradigms.  In the
Box-to-Point workflow, the initial action $a_0$ is a bounding box with random jitter
$\epsilon \sim U(-\delta, \delta)$ to simulate human imprecision.  In the
Sequential-Click workflow, $a_0$ is a centroid click.  All subsequent refinement steps
follow a unified error-driven mechanism based on distance transforms of FN and FP
regions.

A progress-constrained sampling mechanism enforces $\Delta\text{IoU} \geq \tau$
(default $\tau = 0.04$).  If a candidate action fails this test, the simulator
resamples up to $N = 5$ positions.  Trajectories failing a minimum overall IoU
threshold are discarded, yielding a dataset of 449K--519K monotonically improving
sequences.

\subsubsection{Clinical-Fidelity Process Reward Design}

The total reward is the weighted composite:
\[
  R_\text{total} = w_1 \cdot R_\text{fmt} +
    w_2 \cdot \text{clip}\!\left(R_\text{qual} + \lambda_1 R_\text{imp}
      - \lambda_2 R_\text{over} - \lambda_3 R_\text{cost},\; 0,\, 1\right)
\]
where the five components are:
\begin{itemize}
  \item $R_\text{fmt}$: format reward (0.5 for each of: tool invocation and stop action);
  \item $R_\text{qual} = w_\text{iou}\cdot\text{IoU}_\text{final} +
        w_\text{dice}\cdot\text{Dice}_\text{final}$: quality reward;
  \item $R_\text{imp} = \sum_{t=1}^{N-1} \max(0, \text{IoU}_t - \text{IoU}_{t-1})$:
        Progressive Improvement Bonus;
  \item $R_\text{over} = \text{IoU}_\text{max} - \text{IoU}_\text{final}$: Overshoot
        Penalty;
  \item $R_\text{cost}$: linear Tool-Cost Penalty proportional to trajectory length.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Experimental Results}

MedSAM-Agent is evaluated across 21 open-source datasets spanning 6 imaging
modalities.  On the overall average, MedSAM-Agent with MedSAM2 achieves Dice~= 0.794
and IoU~= 0.705, compared to Dice~= 0.776 for UniBiomed~\cite{unibiomed},
Dice~= 0.482 for Citrus-V~\cite{citrusv}, and Dice~= 0.353 for
Seg-R1~\cite{segr1}.  Zero-shot tool generalization experiments reveal that an agent
trained with MedSAM2 trajectories achieves competitive performance with IMISNet and
SAM2 backends at inference time.

IBISAgent commands the largest training corpus in terms of raw image count (456K SFT
samples drawn from a 3.4M-image pool, plus 888K RL samples), reflecting the
data-intensive nature of click-trajectory learning.  MedSAM-Agent operates at a
comparable scale for SFT and employs a carefully curated 9K high-quality subset for
RL.  MedVistaGym/MedVista-R1 works with a substantially smaller curated corpus,
compensating through the richness of multi-tool reasoning trajectories.

\subsection{Action Space Comparison}

The three systems differ most visibly in how they define the agent's action space.
IBISAgent restricts actions to normalized point clicks together with a termination
signal.  This simplicity enables fine-grained spatial reasoning but means the agent
must initiate every segmentation from an empty mask --- a potentially inefficient
starting point for large, well-defined anatomical structures.

MedSAM-Agent addresses this limitation by adding bounding box initialization.  The
ablation in~\cite{medsam_agent} confirms that this addition reduces average trajectory
length from 2.94 to 2.06 steps and improves final IoU from 0.623 to 0.649, with the
hybrid strategy achieving 0.686.  MedVistaGym/MedVista-R1 operates at a qualitatively
different level of abstraction, its action space encompassing structured JSON tool
invocation calls rather than pixel-level spatial coordinates.

\subsection{Reward Function Design}

IBISAgent employs the richest reward signal --- five components covering format, final
accuracy, per-click spatial validity, per-step monotonic improvement, and trajectory
length.  The region-based click placement reward $S_\text{click}$ prevents the agent
from discovering degenerate click policies that happen to produce correct masks by
coincidence.  MedSAM-Agent's Overshoot Penalty $R_\text{over}$ provides a unique form
of stopping criterion supervision not present in the other two systems.
MedVistaGym/MedVista-R1 employs the simplest reward structure, with the conditional
design of $R_\text{tool}$ being its defining innovation.

\subsection{Generalization and Out-of-Domain Performance}

Out-of-domain generalization is the critical test of any segmentation system.
IBISAgent demonstrates particularly impressive zero-shot generalization: on the
held-out in-house dataset drawn from three medical centers and comprising 7 cancer
types, it achieves IoU~= 72.09\%, outperforming all baselines by more than 36
percentage points.  MedSAM-Agent demonstrates zero-shot tool agnosticism --- an agent
trained with MedSAM2 trajectories achieves competitive performance with IMISNet and
SAM2 backends at inference time.  MedVistaGym/MedVista-R1 achieves 52.48\% accuracy
on the OOD average with no out-of-domain samples in training.

\subsection{Computational Overhead}

Multi-turn agentic interaction comes with an unavoidable computational cost.
IBISAgent reports an average inference time of 28.70 seconds per sample, compared to
5.82\,s for UniBiomed and 3.14\,s for MedPLIB, though within clinically acceptable
bounds for non-emergent diagnostic tasks.  MedSAM-Agent achieves an average of 2.11
interaction turns in the full SFT+RL configuration, demonstrating that process rewards
substantially reduce unnecessary interaction steps.  MedVistaGym/MedVista-R1 limits
tool calls to a maximum of 6 per query.

\section{Integrative Synthesis and Future Frontiers}
\label{sec:synthesis}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{Convergent Design Principles}

Despite emerging from independent research groups with different application foci, the
three systems converge on several design principles that appear to reflect genuine
insights about agentic medical AI.

First, all three systems adopt a two-stage training paradigm combining supervised
fine-tuning cold-start with RL.  The SFT stage establishes syntactic and semantic
priors necessary for coherent tool invocation, while RL enables the agent to optimize
for long-horizon outcomes beyond what demonstration learning can achieve.  Across all
three ablation studies, the full SFT+RL combination outperforms either stage alone by
substantial margins.

Second, all three systems treat the observation as a visual object rather than a
symbolic summary.  IBISAgent and MedSAM-Agent overlay the current mask on the original
image, re-encoding this composite through the vision encoder at each step.
MedVistaGym passes processed images (zoomed regions, segmentation masks) alongside
structured text.  This consistent design choice reflects the recognition that
pixel-level reasoning requires continuous visual access to the evolving segmentation
state.

Third, all three papers demonstrate that tool access without disciplined policy
training is insufficient or even harmful.  These convergent findings constitute strong
evidence for the necessity of structured policy training in agentic medical AI --- a
finding that practitioners should carefully consider when designing deployment systems.

\subsection{Open Research Challenges}

Several important challenges remain unresolved across all three systems.  The extension
to 3D volumetric data is acknowledged by all three papers as a priority future
direction.  Extending multi-turn agentic reasoning to 3D will require rethinking the
action space (adding a $z$-coordinate to click prompts), the observation representation
(volumetric mask overlays), and the reward design (3D volumetric IoU rather than
slice-level IoU).

The training data construction bottleneck remains significant.  IBISAgent requires
automated click simulation on 3.4 million annotated images, GPT-5 reasoning synthesis,
and human review of generated traces.  MedSAM-Agent requires trajectory filtering to
ensure monotonic IoU improvement.  MedVistaGym requires live deployment of 15 tool
services during data generation.  Each of these pipelines involves substantial
engineering overhead that may be difficult to replicate in resource-constrained
research settings.

The knowledge gap failure mode identified in MedVistaGym --- where the agent correctly
invokes tools and correctly interprets their outputs but arrives at an incorrect
diagnosis due to incorrect medical concept knowledge --- reveals that tool augmentation
and RL cannot substitute for domain-specific factual grounding.

Finally, none of the three systems has been evaluated in a prospective clinical setting
with real patients and real-time constraints.  Latency guarantees and adaptive stopping
mechanisms will be essential for clinical deployment.

\subsection{Directions for Future Research}

Based on the analysis above, we identify several promising directions for future work.
The unification of point-click and tool-invocation action spaces --- combining
IBISAgent's spatial click precision with MedVistaGym's heterogeneous tool library ---
represents a natural integration that neither system currently realizes.

Process reward design merits further investigation.  MedSAM-Agent's overshoot penalty
and IBISAgent's region-based click placement reward each address qualitatively different
aspects of decision quality.  A more comprehensive process reward that simultaneously
captures click spatial validity, marginal quality improvement, optimal stopping, and
tool selection appropriateness would provide denser training signal throughout the
trajectory.

The development of standardized evaluation protocols across the three systems is
urgently needed.  Currently, IBISAgent, MedVistaGym, and MedSAM-Agent are evaluated
on different benchmarks with different metrics, making direct cross-system comparison
impossible without re-implementing all three systems on a common evaluation suite.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

This survey has examined three independently developed agentic systems for medical
image segmentation and reasoning --- IBISAgent~\cite{ibisagent},
MedVistaGym/MedVista-R1~\cite{medvistagym}, and MedSAM-Agent~\cite{medsam_agent} ---
through a common analytical lens covering conceptual motivation, architectural design,
training methodology, experimental validation, and practical limitations.

The three systems collectively establish that reformulating medical image analysis as
an iterative, feedback-driven decision-making process --- rather than as a static
single-pass prediction --- yields substantial and consistent improvements across diverse
benchmarks and imaging modalities.  The convergent adoption of two-stage SFT+RL
training, visual observation re-encoding, and multi-component reward design across all
three independent projects suggests that these are principled solutions to the
fundamental challenges of agentic clinical AI, not idiosyncratic design choices.

At the same time, the systems differ in important ways that reflect different
prioritizations of clinical realism versus computational tractability.  IBISAgent's
pure point-click action space and rich five-component reward enable exceptionally high
segmentation accuracy but at the cost of longer average trajectories.  MedSAM-Agent's
hybrid box-and-point strategy and process-focused reward achieve excellent efficiency
(2.11 average turns) while maintaining competitive accuracy.  MedVistaGym's
tool-diversification approach enables flexible multi-modal reasoning at the cost of a
more complex action space that requires careful training.

The field stands at an inflection point.  The foundational design principles for
agentic medical image analysis are now established.  The remaining challenges --- 3D
volumetric extension, knowledge grounding, prospective clinical validation, and
standardized evaluation --- represent the next generation of research problems that the
community must address if these systems are to transition from laboratory demonstrations
to clinical tools.
